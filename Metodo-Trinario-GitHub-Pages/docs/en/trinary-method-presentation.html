Trinary
Method

An epistemic, graded, contextual, diagnostic, and revisable logic for decisions under uncertainty, conflict, incomplete data, and continuous updating.

−0.95 to +0.95A revisable scale without absolute truth or falsity.
0A state of insufficient knowledge, conflict, or inadequate formulation.
Weighted meanPositive and negative results are aggregated without artificial raw counting.
1. Origin and purpose

A practical response to binary limits

The Trinary Method was created to support simple and effective decisions without forcing every evaluation into true or false.

Problem

Binary decisions can force conclusions when information is insufficient, contradictory, ambiguous, or temporarily incomplete.

Proposal

Represent not only the direction of a conclusion, but also its intensity, coverage, conflict, context, and revisability.

Core principle: not proven is not false; not refuted is not true; not knowing is a legitimate state.
2. Fundamental semantics

Three regions, infinitely many internal degrees

The logic is trinary because it has three fundamental states: negative, zero, and positive. Negative and positive fields are graded.

−0.95 to < 0Graded refutation
0Indeterminacy
> 0 to +0.95Graded support

Negative

Evidence favors rejection. Values nearer −0.95 indicate stronger refutation.

Zero

The system suspends judgment because of absence, insufficiency, conflict, ambiguity, or a pending condition.

Positive

Evidence favors the proposition. Values nearer +0.95 indicate stronger support.

For every assessable proposition φ: −0.95 ≤ v(φ) ≤ +0.95

Values ±1 are excluded because absolute certainty conflicts with a revisable logic.

3. Diagnosing zero

Complete response: R(φ) = value + diagnosis + explanation
4. Evidence mechanism

Weighted mean, coverage, and conflict

Positive and negative outcomes are aggregated through weighted means so numerous weak observations do not artificially dominate a few strong ones.

Evidence weight

Total weight may include quality, reliability, independence, recency, relevance, and consistency.

weight = quality × reliability × independence × recency × relevance × consistency

Weighted mean

w₁x₁ + w₂x₂ + ... + wₙxₙw₁ + w₂ + ... + wₙ

The same procedure is applied separately to favorable and contrary evidence.

M⁺

Weighted mean of favorable evidence.

M⁻

Weighted mean of the magnitudes of contrary evidence.

Coverage

Breadth, methodological diversity, independence, and completeness of inquiry.

E⁺ = M⁺ × C⁺     and     E⁻ = M⁻ × C⁻
Primary result: R = E⁺ − E⁻
Conflict index K = 2 × min(E⁺, E⁻)E⁺ + E⁻
5. Logical connectives

Graded and cautious operations

OperationInitial ruleInterpretation
Negationv(¬φ) = −v(φ)Reverses direction and preserves magnitude.
Conjunctionminimum valueThe joint conclusion cannot exceed its weakest premise.
Disjunctionmaximum valueThe best-supported alternative prevails.
Implicationstrength bounded by premise and rulePrevents unjustified amplification.
Equivalencebased on value proximityCloser assessments imply stronger equivalence.
These operations form the first operational version and require formal and experimental validation.
6. Inference rules

Conclusions are never stronger than their bases

Identity
A proposition keeps its value while context and evidence remain unchanged.
Negation
Only the sign changes.
Graded modus ponens
The conclusion is bounded by the smaller value of premise and implication.
Zero blocking
An indeterminate premise does not license a positive or negative conclusion.
No amplification
No derived conclusion exceeds the weakest essential premise or rule.
Non-explosive contradiction
From φ and ¬φ, an arbitrary proposition does not follow.
Revisability
New evidence may strengthen, weaken, or reverse a conclusion.
7. Logical value and practical decision

Logic informs; policy decides

A positive or negative value does not automatically authorize action. Decisions also depend on sufficiency, conflict, risk, cost, reversibility, and domain rules.

Evidence→Weighted means→Coverage→Conflict→Trinary value→Decision policy→Action or suspension

Approve

Sufficient positive value, adequate coverage, acceptable conflict, and controlled risk.

Reject

Sufficient negative value, adequate coverage, acceptable conflict, and authorizing policy.

Suspend

Near-zero value, low coverage, high conflict, ambiguity, or pending condition.

8. Complete example

“The equipment can operate safely.”

Positive mean: 0.813

Positive coverage: 0.85

Effective positive support: 0.691

Negative mean: 0.509

Negative coverage: 0.45

Effective negative support: 0.229

Result: 0.462

Conservative sufficiency: 0.65

Final value: +0.30

Conflict: approximately 0.50

Decision: do not release automatically; investigate vibration and repeat the test.

9. Practical applications

Where the method may outperform binary decisions

Artificial Intelligence

Declares insufficiency, conflict, and need for more data instead of fabricating answers.

Medicine

Separates diagnostic evidence, test coverage, clinical conflict, and therapeutic decisions.

Law

Distinguishes favorable and contrary evidence, insufficiency, conflict, and decision thresholds.

Robotics

Combines sensors with different reliability and avoids action under inconsistent information.

Databases

Represents missing information without automatically treating it as false.

Science

Formalizes revisable hypotheses, evidence strength, experimental conflict, and suspended judgment.
