Proposal for a formal logical system

Trinary Logic E?

Three-valued semantics, epistemological metadata, truth tables, inference calculus, and consistency results
Author: Arthur Braga Padilha
Abstract

This document formalizes Trinary Logic E? as a three-valued propositional system. T and F represent truth and falsity; E represents evaluative indeterminacy. The human-facing symbol E? may carry explanatory metadata: insufficient data, conflict or malformed formulation, or intangibility. The base semantics is compositional, non-explosive, and conservative over classical logic whenever no formula receives E.

This is a mathematical proposal defined in this document. It is not, by itself, proof of historical originality and does not replace academic comparison with existing three-valued logics.

1. Objective and scope

The system answers two separate questions:

  1. What is the semantic state of the proposition? — T, F, or E.
  2. Why is judgment suspended? — metadata associated with E.

This distinction prevents epistemological causes from becoming additional truth values. The logical core has exactly three values; causes of E belong to a diagnostic layer.

2. Formal language

2.1 Alphabet

Let Prop = {p₁,p₂,p₃,…} be a countable set of propositional variables. Language LE contains variables, connectives ¬, ∧, ∨, →, ↔, and parentheses.

2.2 Formation

Definition 1 — Formulas
φ ::= p | ¬φ | (φ ∧ φ) | (φ ∨ φ) | (φ → φ) | (φ ↔ φ).

3. Semantic domain

Definition 2 — Values
𝕍 = { F, E, T }

Use the order:

F < E < T.

T — True

Current evidence and interpretation license acceptance.

F — False

Current evidence and interpretation license rejection.

E — E?

There is no responsible semantic authorization to assert T or F.

3.1 E? metadata

Definition 3 — Diagnosis

If v(φ)=E, associate:

δ(φ) ∈ { D, C, I }
  • D — Insufficient data: assessable in principle, but decisive data is missing.
  • C — Conflict or malformed formulation: contradiction, material ambiguity, pathological self-reference, or a poorly specified domain.
  • I — Intangible: no objective decision procedure is available in the adopted framework.

The diagnosis explains E but does not change truth tables.

5. Logical truth and consequence

Definition 5 — Designated value

The only designated value is T.

D = {T}.
Definition 6 — Semantic consequence
Γ ⊨E φ ⇔ for every valuation v, if v(γ)=T for every γ∈Γ, then v(φ)=T.
Definition 7 — Validity
⊨E φ ⇔ v(φ)=T for every valuation v.
Important consequence

p∨¬p is not generally valid: when v(p)=E, it evaluates to E. Classical laws are preserved in the bivalent fragment.

6. Inference calculus

A natural-deduction presentation is adopted with rules that safely preserve designated value T.

6.1 Structural rules

Identity: Γ,φ ⊢ φ
Weakening: if Γ ⊢ φ, then Γ,ψ ⊢ φ
Cut: if Γ ⊢ ψ and Γ,ψ ⊢ φ, then Γ ⊢ φ

6.2 Conjunction

∧I: from φ and ψ, infer φ∧ψ.
∧E₁: from φ∧ψ, infer φ.
∧E₂: from φ∧ψ, infer ψ.

6.3 Disjunction

∨I₁: from φ, infer φ∨ψ.
∨I₂: from ψ, infer φ∨ψ.
∨E: from φ∨ψ, [φ ⊢ χ], and [ψ ⊢ χ], infer χ.

6.4 Implication

→I: if ψ is derived under assumption φ, infer φ→ψ and discharge φ.
→E (Modus Ponens): from φ and φ→ψ, infer ψ.

6.5 Negation

Negation reverses T/F and preserves E. Unrestricted explosion φ,¬φ ⊢ ψ is not adopted.

6.6 Diagnostic suspension rule

Rule E?
If φ cannot be justified as T or F, record Eval(φ)=E and choose δ(φ)∈{D,C,I}.

This operational rule does not authorize an arbitrary inference from E.

7. Formal properties

Theorem 1 — Conservativity over the classical fragment

If a valuation assigns only T or F to the variables of φ, then φ has the same value in Trinary Logic E? and classical logic.

Proof

By structural induction. Negation, minimum, and maximum restricted to {F,T} coincide with classical connectives; implication and biconditional are defined from them. ∎

Theorem 2 — Soundness of the main rules

The conjunction, disjunction, and Modus Ponens rules preserve T.

Proof

Directly from the truth tables and the definition of semantic consequence. ∎

Theorem 3 — Non-explosion

Operational contradiction does not license arbitrary conclusions.

Proof

Conflicting evidence is classified as E/C; no rule derives an arbitrary ψ from that diagnostic state. ∎

Theorem 4 — Relative syntactic consistency

If classical logic is consistent, the classical fragment is consistent, and the complete calculus is non-trivial.

Proof

Conservativity transfers classical consistency. A valuation with v(p)=T and v(q)=F shows q cannot be a theorem under soundness. ∎

Theorem 5 — Monotonicity

If Γ ⊨E φ and Γ⊆Δ, then Δ ⊨E φ.

8. Epistemological classification protocol

  1. Well-formedness: are syntax and domain defined?
  2. Coherence: is there internal conflict or pathological self-reference?
  3. Practical decidability: are data or tests available?
  4. Evaluation: assign T, F, or E.
  5. Diagnosis: when E, record D, C, or I.

11. Limitations and open questions

  • “Intangible” depends on the epistemological framework and may change with new methods.
  • C currently combines conflict and malformed formulation; future versions may separate them.
  • The present semantics is propositional; first-order extension requires domains, quantifiers, and additional rules.
  • A belief-revision theory must define transitions from E/D to T or F.
  • Explicitly inconsistent databases may benefit from a four-valued paraconsistent extension.

12. Formal verdict

Conclusion

Trinary Logic E? can be formalized as a three-valued propositional logic with one designated value, compositional connectives, and an external diagnostic layer. It is conservative over classical logic in the bivalent fragment, preserves Modus Ponens and standard conjunction/disjunction rules, is non-trivial, and explicitly represents suspended judgment.

Its main contribution is not to refute binary logic, but to broaden operational reasoning: accept, reject, or formally record indeterminacy and its cause.
